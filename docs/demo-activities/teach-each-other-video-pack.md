# Teach-each-other video demo pack

These activities are designed for Junto's **Teach each other** grouping approach. Each activity has one focused
question, five atomic coverage units, and several legitimate response families.

## Recommended settings

- Grouping approach: **Teach each other**
- Simulated students: **10**
- Minimum group size: **3**
- Preferred group size: **3**
- Maximum group size: **4**
- Duration: **10 minutes**

Ten students produce three groups of 4, 3, and 3. The short reference material supplies evidence without spelling out
the coverage rubric, allowing simulated students to contribute different subsets of ideas.

Only enter the title, reference material, question, and coverage units into Junto. The expected response families and
video notes are presenter guidance; Junto should discover the families from the answers.

# Activity 1: Why did Western Rome fall?

## Activity title

Why did Western Rome fall?

## Reference material

During the fifth century, the Western Roman Empire experienced repeated civil wars and rapid changes of emperor. These
conflicts disrupted government and consumed money needed for defense. At the same time, the empire lost tax-paying land
and struggled to support its armies. Gothic forces entered Roman territory, Rome was sacked in 410, and the Vandals
captured important provinces. The Eastern Roman Empire faced many external threats too, but survived with a stronger tax
base, more stable administration, and a more defensible capital.

## Question

Which explanation is strongest: internal weakness, external pressure, or an interaction of both? Support your view with
the reference material.

## Coverage units

1. Political instability weakened the western government
2. Lost tax revenue reduced the empire's ability to defend itself
3. Invasions and territorial losses increased external pressure
4. Internal weakness and external pressure reinforced each other
5. The Eastern Empire's survival challenges a simple single-cause explanation

## Expected response families — presenter guidance only

- Internal weakness was the main cause
- External pressure was the main cause
- Internal and external causes interacted

## Why the grouping should be interesting

Some answers should focus on civil wars and money, others on invasions, and stronger answers may connect the two. The
Eastern comparison is likely to be covered by only part of the cohort, giving Junto a useful contribution to distribute.

# Activity 2: Betrayal or resistance?

## Activity title

Betrayal or resistance?

## Reference material

At dusk, Leena entered the mayor's archive with the key she had sworn to return. She slipped the eviction ledger into
her coat. When the church bell sounded, she paused beneath her father's portrait, then turned it to the wall. "Not for
me," she said, and carried the ledger to the families waiting at the river. Years later she remembered the bell, though
no one agreed whether it rang before or after she took the book. In the cracked window, her reflection appeared doubled.

## Question

Is Leena's action best understood as betrayal, resistance, or a mixture of both? Support your interpretation with the
passage.

## Coverage units

1. Taking the ledger to the families gives the action a public purpose
2. The broken promise or hidden portrait suggests guilt
3. Taking the ledger can be understood as resistance to authority
4. The disputed timing of the bell limits certainty about Leena's motive
5. The doubled reflection suggests mixed motives or inner conflict

## Expected response families — presenter guidance only

- Betrayal is the main interpretation
- Resistance is the main interpretation
- Betrayal and resistance are both important
- The passage is too ambiguous for one primary interpretation

## Why the grouping should be interesting

Students can reach different conclusions while citing overlapping evidence. A betrayal answer may contribute the broken
promise, a resistance answer may contribute the families' needs, and an ambiguity-focused answer may contribute the bell
or doubled reflection.

# Activity 3: Counting robot routes with dynamic programming

## Activity title

Counting robot routes with dynamic programming

## Reference material

A robot begins at cell 0 and must reach cell `n` on a straight line. On each move it may advance by one or two cells.
Some cells are blocked, and the robot may not land on them. Cell 0 and cell `n` are not blocked. The goal is to count
the number of valid routes without listing every possible route.

## Question

Explain one efficient way to count the valid routes, including its time and space cost.

## Coverage units

1. Defines a state as the number of routes reaching a cell
2. Uses one route at cell 0 as the base case
3. Gives blocked cells zero routes
4. Combines counts from one-step and two-step moves
5. Gives linear time and explains the storage cost

## Expected response families — presenter guidance only

- Top-down recursion with memoization
- Bottom-up dynamic-programming table
- Forward propagation from each reachable cell

## Why the grouping should be interesting

Some answers should contain only the recurrence, others the base or blocked-cell rule, and others a complete complexity
analysis. Different implementations can cover the same core ideas while remaining separate response families.

# Suggested recording order

1. Record **Betrayal or resistance?** first if you want the clearest visible disagreement.
2. Record **Counting robot routes** to show that Junto also works for objective technical learning.
3. Publish **Why did Western Rome fall?** to add a causal-evidence activity to the Activities page.

For the recording, open one resulting group and point out three things:

1. members used different overall approaches or interpretations;
2. different members carry different coverage units;
3. the group agenda turns those contributions into a concrete discussion plan.
